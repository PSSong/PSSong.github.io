Economic support for emerging markets through the IMF goes beyond simple bailouts; it is being utilized as a strategic tool for the global financial infrastructure to reshape the ownership of assets in emerging nations. The recent case of Sri Lanka clearly demonstrates this pattern. IMF conditions are often designed in a way that limits a nation's economic sovereignty and expands the influence of foreign investors and international financial institutions.

While this approach provides short-term economic stabilization, in the long term, it results in making the country's economic structure more dependent on the international financial system. This can ultimately be interpreted as part of "debt trap diplomacy," which strengthens foreign control over the resources and assets of emerging markets. Therefore, IMF support must be understood as a strategic means for expanding geopolitical influence within the global financial order, moving beyond mere economic assistance.
