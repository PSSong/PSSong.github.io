Since the 1st, geopolitical instability related to Iran's military movements has been escalating. The threat of a blockade in the Strait of Hormuz is amplifying concerns regarding the global energy supply chain, acting as a factor that sustains inflationary pressures. However, this instability goes beyond a mere natural market reaction and is linked to a broader monetary policy strategy.

The Federal Reserve (Fed) appears to be leveraging geopolitical instability to control inflation. The negative economic impacts of external factors, such as war risks, help curb inflation, thereby fostering an environment where there is no rush to implement interest rate cuts. This can be interpreted as a strategy to secure the time necessary for the Fed to stabilize inflation toward its target level.

In this context, geopolitical crises are not merely crises in themselves; they are being utilized as tools for economic policy. Therefore, market participants must look beyond the nature of geopolitical instability and focus on how it is being reflected in monetary policy.
